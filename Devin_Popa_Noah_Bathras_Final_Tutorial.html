Introduction¶

In attempts to make movies bigger, more exciting, and innovative, budgets for Hollywood productions and independent films alike are increasing. This measn that movies which find success through brand-name recognition or word of mouth and/or viral marketing campaigns have the opportunity to earn a lot of money if they make their budget back. On the other hand, movies that fail to break even appear to fail more spectacularly than they had in years past. While it is a binary scale that is not completely indicative of a movie's success, for the purposes of this tutorial, we will define a movie's success based on whether or not it grossed enough money in total to earn its budget back for the sake of simplicity.

Building on this definition, we will examine the business side of movies through data analysis and various machine learning algorithms to make an attempt to predict whether or not movies will be successful. Our models will consider a film's distribution studio, release date, MPAA rating, and genre from movies released between the years of 2015 and 2018 to see whether or not a film is likely to make its budget back. Prior to this, we will be collecting and tidying the information we gather from our data source and graphing plots for noteworthy trends between the previously mentioned factors and film box office gross.

While such an analysis might only seem useful for the people involved in the production of films, avid moviegoers can take away a lot of information from this as well. It is natural to assume that movie studios will continue to endorse projects that are known to be popular or well-received, and they may be less open to doing the same for unknown quantities. Going by this, people who are interested can theorize the types of films that will be released in the recent future. Finally, those that are interested in creating their own films may get a better sense of what types of movies are "safe bets" among audiences, and which ones may be riskier.

Limitations¶

Before beginning this tutuorial, we should discuss some limitations of our analysis. As our previous definition of success would suggest, we do not take critical recpetion or word-of-mouth as factors for our machine learning models. Part of this could be alleviated by using the Twitter API to count the number of times a hashtag associated with a movie was used during its theatrical run, but this would not determine the positive or negative valence of the tweets being examined. It should also be noted that we are only looking at films released from 2015 to 2018, so a larger sample of data, or a different subset of movie data across four years, may yield different results than the ones found here.

Data Collection¶

All of the data that we have collected comes from https://www.the-numbers.com/, a website that manages a large database of movies and creates tables for different sets of properties and films. For example, the domestic gross information for movies screened in 2018 can be found here: https://www.the-numbers.com/market/2018/top-grossing-movies. To find the information for each year between 2015 and 2018, we first created a function that would scrape data from a single web page. We store the data into an object called a DataFrame, which can be thought of as the Pandas equivalent to a table.

def getSingleYearAnnualTopGrossingMovies(url):
    # Make a web request to the url parameter
    req = requests.get(url)
    req.encoding = 'utf-8' # guarantees movie titles are displayed correctly 
    soup = BeautifulSoup(req.text)

    # Remove all links/tags from the entries
    table = soup.find('table')
    for a in table.findAll('a'):
        a.replaceWithChildren() 

    # Create a new DataFrame from the table variable
    df_list = pd.read_html(str(table))
    movie_df = df_list[0]
    movie_df.columns = ['rank', 'movie', 'releaseDate', 'distributor', 
                        'genre', 'rating', 'domesticGross', 'ticketsSold']
    movie_df = movie_df.drop([0])     # drop the first row, does not include data
    movie_df = movie_df[:-2]     # drop the last two rows, contain aggregate data
    # removes any rows that do not have data in them
    movie_df = movie_df.dropna()
    
    return movie_df

Now that we are done scraping the gross data for each year, we need to collect data on the budgets of these movies. The budget data (found at https://www.the-numbers.com/movie/budgets/all) that this site gives operates differently than the domestic gross data. Instead of creating separate tables for each year, the budget data is a large data table that contains movies from any year separated into pages that each contain 100 entries. We start with the base page of budget data and perform a similar process of collecting data seen for the gross web pages, with an additional step of tidying the data in order to convert all of the monetary information from strings to integer values. This will make it easier to make comparisons between movies for each of these values and make later analysis easier.

This process only provides the data for one page, though, and so we repeat the same steps for the next 55 pages of movies. To do this, we store the indices of 101 to 5601 in increments of 100, each of which form part of the link to a new page of the film budget table. We then repeat the steps used to scrape data from the first page within a loop over each of the indices to create a new dataframe containing the newly accessed data. At the end of the loop, we append this new dataframe to the existing one to maintain a collection of all of the values gathered, then move on to the next index until we have scraped data from all of the pages.

# Request to initial budget data
def getMovieBudgetData():
    req = requests.get('https://www.the-numbers.com/movie/budgets/all')
    req.encoding = 'utf-8'
    soup = BeautifulSoup(req.text)

    # Create new DataFrame in i similar process as before
    table = soup.find('table')
    for a in table.findAll('a'):
        a.replaceWithChildren()

    df_list = pd.read_html(str(table))
    budget_df = df_list[0]
    budget_df.columns = ['id', 'releaseDate', 'movie', 'budget', 'domesticGross', 'totalGross']
    budget_df = budget_df.drop(['id', 'domesticGross'], axis=1)    # drop id column
    budget_df = budget_df.drop([0])    # drop one unnecessary row
    budget_df = budget_df.dropna()     # drop any missing data

    # Conversion of moonetary values given as strings to integers
    budgetConvert = []
    totalGrossConvert = []
    for index, row in budget_df.iterrows():
        converted_budget = int(row["budget"][1:].replace(",", ""))
        budgetConvert.append(converted_budget)
        converted_total = int(row['totalGross'][1:].replace(',', ''))
        totalGrossConvert.append(converted_total)
    budget_df["budget"] = budgetConvert
    budget_df['totalGross'] = totalGrossConvert

    # Store additional numbers as strings that will help link to each additional page
    # of 100 entries
    pages = []
    index = 101
    while index <= 5601:
        pages.append(str(index))
        index += 100

    # Repeat previous steps in retrieving budget data, but with additional entries that have separate links
    # WARNING: Causes long load times when recollecting the data
    for index in pages:
        req = requests.get('https://www.the-numbers.com/movie/budgets/all' + '/' + index)
        req.encoding = 'utf-8'
        soup = BeautifulSoup(req.text)

        table = soup.find('table')
        for a in table.findAll('a'):
            a.replaceWithChildren()

        df_list = pd.read_html(str(table))
        other_budget_df = df_list[0]
        other_budget_df.columns = ['id', 'releaseDate', 'movie', 'budget', 'domesticGross', 'totalGross']
        other_budget_df = other_budget_df.drop(['id', 'domesticGross'], axis=1)    # drop id column
        other_budget_df = other_budget_df.drop([0])     # drop one unnecessary row
        other_budget_df = other_budget_df.dropna()     # drop any missing data


        budgetConvert = []
        totalGrossConvert = []
        for index, row in other_budget_df.iterrows():
            converted_budget = int(row["budget"][1:].replace(",", ""))
            budgetConvert.append(converted_budget)
            converted_total = int(row['totalGross'][1:].replace(',', ''))
            totalGrossConvert.append(converted_total)
        other_budget_df["budget"] = budgetConvert
        other_budget_df['totalGross'] = totalGrossConvert

        budget_df = budget_df.append(other_budget_df)

    # New DataFrame containing budget and worldwide gross (not just domestic, unlike previous dataframe)
    # information for a large selection of movies
    return budget_df

Tidying The Data¶

Now that we have collected all of the data we will be using, the next step is to further prepare it for analysis by making small adjustments. The first of these is to combine the two separate dataframes for gross and budget information into one. We achieve this through an inner join, which means that any movies that are in one table but not the other will not be added to the combined dataframe. In a later step, we will change this process to use a left join, which combines the two tables based on the movies found in the movie DataFrame. It is worth noting that a left join can lead to missing values in the combined DataFrame. An example of the different types of join operations that can be performed, as well as a visual representation of these types, can be found here: https://www.shanelynn.ie/merge-join-dataframes-python-pandas-index-1/.

def combineMovieAndBudgetDF(movie_df, budget_df, how='inner'):
    movie_df = movie_df.merge(budget_df, on=['movie', 'releaseDate'], how=how)
    return movie_df

After joining our two datasets together, we need to perform a few more numeric conversions on the rank of a film's gross (from highest to lowest), the number of tickets a film sold, and its annual domestic gross. Additionally, we split the release date for each film by month and year so that we can perform comparisons between a movie's domestic gross and both the month and year it was released.

# Final reformat of the data
def reformatData(movie_df):
    domesticGrossConvert = []
    monthList = []
    yearList  = []
    for index, row in movie_df.iterrows():
        # converts annual gross to a number
        converted_string = int(row["domesticGross"][1:].replace(",", ""))
        domesticGrossConvert.append(converted_string)
        
        # converts dates to two columns and numbers
        month_int = int(row["releaseDate"].split("/")[0])
        monthList.append(month_int)
        year_int  = int(row["releaseDate"].split("/")[2])
        yearList.append(year_int)
    movie_df["releaseMonth"]  = monthList
    movie_df["releaseYear"]   = yearList
    movie_df["domesticGross"] = domesticGrossConvert

    # converts ticketsSold to numbers
    movie_df["ticketsSold"] = pd.to_numeric(movie_df["ticketsSold"])

    # converts rank to numbers
    movie_df["rank"] = pd.to_numeric(movie_df["rank"])
    
    # sorts based on rank
    movie_df = movie_df.sort_values(by=['rank'])
    
    # resets index values again
    movie_df = movie_df.reset_index(drop=True)
    
    return movie_df

Data Visualization¶

Before we begin to test classification of movies as successful or unsuccessful, we will begin our data analysis by visualizing the current trends of movies based on the characteristics that are included with the data. First we will be examining the portion of the domestic gross each genre of movie has. While the genres that are earning the largest amounts of money are not surprising, the extent to which they dominate the market is. Action and adventure films alone account for more than half of the money earned by all films, with action movies earning only 0.07%. The next most significant portion of domestic gross is held by drama films, but these comprise 14.06% of money earned by films, or half of either action or adventure movies alone. These trends make sense to cultural expectations, as the movies that people are most likely to see are either "summer blockbusters" or "Oscar bait movies".

The other important trend to note is that movies labeled under the genres of reality, multiple genres, and concert/performance films all have a combined share of less than 0.1% of the money earned by all movies. One explanation for this could that the number of movies that were made having these specific genres identifying them is likely very low, as multiple genres doesn't seem like something people would want to advertise a movie as due to its vague description. This is supported by the fact that other movies with niche genres like black comedies and westerns are faring mushc worse than movies of more conventional styles. Alternatively, as is the case with concert and/or performance films, these films might get limited relesases in theaters for one-night-only showings, which will dramatically reduce the amount of money such a movie is able to earn.

Creating Feature Lists¶

Now that we have the data in a form that allows for comparisons between different pieces of information, we can start analyzing a movie's chance of success based on these criteria. We will start by defining our feature lists, which consist of genre, rating, distributor, release month and year, domestic gross, and budget. To start, we create a dictionary for each categorical attribute. Then, for every unique value of an entry in the DataFrame, we add a key to the corresponding attribute DataFrame for that possibility, and its associated value is a list of numbers equal to the number of total entries in the DataFrame. The list is initialized to zeros. For quantitative attributes, we skip the creation of the dictionary and use a list initialized to all zeros for every row of the DataFrame.

def build_default_feature_lists(df):
    num_rows = len(df.index)

    # genre dictionary creation
#     print(movie_df.genre.unique())
    genre_name_list = ['Action', 'Adventure', 'Musical', 'Drama', 'Horror',
                       'Thriller/Suspense', 'Comedy', 'Romantic Comedy', 'Western',
                       'Black Comedy', 'Concert/Performance']
    genre_name_dict = {}

    # Add key for each genre_name to genre_dict
    for genre_name in genre_name_list:
        default_list                = np.zeros(num_rows)
        genre_name_dict[genre_name] = default_list
    default_list             = np.zeros(num_rows)
    genre_name_dict["other"] = default_list

    # rating dictionary creation
#     print(movie_df.rating.unique())
    rating_name_list = ['PG-13', 'PG', 'R', 'G']
    rating_name_dict = {}

    # Add key for each rating to rating_name_dict
    for rating_name in rating_name_list:
        default_list                  = np.zeros(num_rows)
        rating_name_dict[rating_name] = default_list
    default_list              = np.zeros(num_rows)
    rating_name_dict["other"] = default_list

    # distributor dictionary creation
#     print(movie_df.distributor.unique())
    distributor_name_list = list(df.distributor.unique())
    distributor_name_list = distributor_name_list[:20]

    distributor_name_dict = {}

    for distributor_name in distributor_name_list:
        default_list                            = np.zeros(num_rows)
        distributor_name_dict[distributor_name] = default_list
    default_list                   = np.zeros(num_rows)
    distributor_name_dict["other"] = default_list

    # basic list creation
    releaseMonth_list  = np.zeros(num_rows)
    releaseYear_list   = np.zeros(num_rows)

    domesticGross_list = np.zeros(num_rows)
    budget_list        = np.zeros(num_rows)
    
    return (genre_name_dict, rating_name_dict, distributor_name_dict, releaseMonth_list, releaseYear_list, domesticGross_list, budget_list)

The next step is to construct two arrays. X will contain lists for each attribute we want to examine. The values for each list will be the dictionaries defined from the previous function, if we are looking at a qualitative factor, or the list of numbers retrieved for a numeric variable. The exact steps to obtain these arrays are left as comments in the code below, but once we have them, we will be able to examine the accuracy with which this information allows us to predict the success of a movie.

def build_feature_lists(df, is_include_budget=False, is_classification=False):
    genre_name_dict, rating_name_dict, distributor_name_dict, releaseMonth_list, releaseYear_list, domesticGross_list, budget_list = build_default_feature_lists(df)

    counter = 0
    for index, row in df.iterrows():
        
        # Get value for each category to train on
        domesticGross_value = row["domesticGross"]
        genre_value         = row["genre"]
        distributor_value   = row["distributor"]
        rating_value        = row["rating"]
        releaseMonth_value  = row["releaseMonth"]
        releaseYear_value   = row["releaseYear"]

        # Increment counter for categorical values if a known option was found
        # Otherwise, increment counter for other
        if genre_value in genre_name_dict:
            genre_name_dict[genre_value][counter] = 1
        else:
            genre_name_dict["other"][counter] = 1

        if rating_value in rating_name_dict:
            rating_name_dict[rating_value][counter] = 1
        else:
            rating_name_dict["other"][counter] = 1

        if distributor_value in distributor_name_dict:
            distributor_name_dict[distributor_value][counter] = 1
        else:
            distributor_name_dict["other"][counter] = 1

        # Add numerical data for release month and year as is
        releaseMonth_list[counter] = int(releaseMonth_value)
        releaseYear_list[counter]  = int(releaseYear_value)

        # Get budget value if applicable
        budget_value = None
        if is_include_budget:
            budget_value        = row["budget"]
            budget_list[counter] = int(budget_value)        
        
        # Check for success in earning back budget with domestic gross
        if is_classification:
            if domesticGross_value > budget_value:
                domesticGross_list[counter] = 1
            else:
                domesticGross_list[counter] = 0
        else:
            domesticGross_list[counter] = int(domesticGross_value)

        # Increment the counter and re-enter the loop
        counter = counter + 1

    # Initializes a DataFrame for training data
    df_train_values = pd.DataFrame(releaseMonth_list, columns=["releaseMonth"])

    # Add new column to training DF for each atttribute and subcategory
    # Use the previously defined lists for numerical data
    df_train_values["releaseYear"] = releaseYear_list
    for key, value in genre_name_dict.items():
        column_name = "genre_" + str(key)
        df_train_values[column_name] = value
    for key, value in rating_name_dict.items():
        column_name = "rating_" + str(key)
        df_train_values[column_name] = value
    for key, value in distributor_name_dict.items():
        column_name = "distributor_" + str(key)
        df_train_values[column_name] = value
    df_train_values["domesticGross"] = domesticGross_list
    if is_include_budget:
        df_train_values["budget"] = budget_list

    # Construct X (the categories that we want to examine for correlation)
    # and y (domestic gross, the category that we are examining to measure success)
    # from the data
    counter = 0
    X = None
    y = np.array(df_train_values["domesticGross"])
    prev_column = None
    new_column  = None
    for column in df_train_values:
        if column == "domesticGross":
            continue

        new_column = df_train_values[column]
        new_column = new_column[:,np.newaxis]
        if counter == 0:
            X = new_column
        else:
            X = np.append(X, new_column, 1)
        counter = counter + 1
    
    return (X,y)

Conclusion¶

Through our visual analysis of the data, we were able to find out about the oversaturation of dramatic and unrated films in the box office market. However, this overwhelming presence in the market may be detrimental to the success of these types of movies, as the average gross for dramas and the total gross for unrated films are both very low for their market presence. On the flip side, action/adventure movies that are rated either PG or PG-13 have the greatest chance to win big with box office earnings even if they aren'the most prevalent types of films on the market. Importantly, filmmakers who want to tell a more unique story should not be worried by the dominance of action/adventure movies, becuase despite the lesser value of total gross for genres like musical and horror films, these types of movies do well on average individually. The last important factor to note is that the prototypical movie should be released when its prototypical teenage audience is out of school in the early-mid summer months, or during the holidays.

The machine learning models we tested taught us a more valuable lesson, and that is that the factors that we looked at within this project are not enough on their own to predict the ability of a movie to earn its budget back with any meaningful accuracy. The random forest classifier was the best performing one of the four main ones that we tried, but it still had an accuracy not much higher than a good guess. Our lack of inclusion of critical response and/or marketing success could have been an important factor as to why our classification models were unhelpful. Additionally, the linear regression models we attempted to fit were not suited to the problem we were trying to address, which suggests two things: 1) that predicting movie performance at the box office is more complicated than a linear relationship of multiple variables, and 2) that the variables we are looking into are not the ones that are going to be the most helpful when determining whether or not a movie can earn its budget back.
